5 Places You Must Install CCTV Cameras at Home

10 Jun 2025

Your home has a handful of vulnerable spots that burglars check first. Covering these with cameras doesn't just capture footage — it actively deters break-ins. Here are the five positions every South African homeowner should prioritise.

1. Front door and main entrance

Over 30% of burglars enter through the front door. A visible camera here serves as both a deterrent and an identification tool. Position it at around 2.5 metres height, angled slightly downward so faces are captured clearly.

2. Back door and kitchen entrance

The second most common entry point. Many homeowners forget this one because it's not visible from the street. A camera here catches anyone approaching from the rear of the property — where most break-ins in South Africa actually happen.

3. Garage and driveway

If your car is outside or your garage is attached to the house, this is a priority. Cameras with night vision and motion detection are essential here — most vehicle and tool theft happens after dark.

4. Side gates and passages

The narrow passages between houses are blind spots that burglars use to move between the front and back of properties undetected. A camera with a wide-angle lens positioned at the passage entry covers this vulnerability.

5. Garden perimeter and wall line

For properties with boundary walls or fences, perimeter cameras catch movement before intruders reach the house. These are especially effective when paired with motion-triggered lighting.

What to look for in cameras

  • Night vision — infrared or colour night vision for after-dark coverage
  • Weatherproof rating — IP65 or higher for South African conditions
  • Remote access — smartphone app so you can check in from anywhere
  • Battery backup — keeps recording during load shedding

Why Smart Alarms Are Replacing Traditional Systems in SA

3 Jun 2025

The alarm systems South Africans grew up with — keypads by the front door, a siren on the roof, panic buttons on the bedside table — are still the backbone of home security. But smart alarm systems have added capabilities that traditional setups can't match.

What's changed?

Traditional alarms rely on a phone line or radio signal to reach a monitoring centre. Smart alarms add cellular (SIM) and WiFi connectivity, which means they work even when Telkom lines are down. They also connect directly to your smartphone, so you get instant push notifications instead of waiting for the control room to call you.

Key advantages of smart alarms

  • Remote arming/disarming — forgot to arm the alarm when you left? Do it from your phone.
  • Zone-by-zone control — arm the perimeter while keeping interior zones off, perfect for when you're home at night.
  • False alarm filtering — smart sensors distinguish between pets, wind, and actual intrusion.
  • Integration — connects to cameras, electric fencing, and access control from a single dashboard.

Are traditional alarms obsolete?

No. A traditional alarm with armed response is still better than no alarm at all. But if you're installing a new system or upgrading, smart is the way to go — the monthly monitoring costs are similar, and the features are significantly better.

Load shedding compatibility

Most smart alarm panels include a battery backup that keeps the system running during power outages. At EE Solutions, every system we install includes backup power as standard.

10 Simple Things That Make Your Home a Harder Target

28 May 2025

You don't need to spend thousands to improve your home security. Many of the most effective measures cost little or nothing — they just require a shift in habits.

  • Lock gates and doors — every time. Unlocked gates and doors are the most common way burglars enter properties in South Africa. It sounds obvious, but it's the number one vulnerability.
  • Don't leave garage doors open. An open garage is an invitation. Even if you're just popping inside for a few minutes, close it.
  • Trim overgrown bushes near windows. Dense vegetation provides cover for someone trying to break in. Keep bushes below window height.
  • Install motion-sensor lights. A sudden burst of light is one of the most effective deterrents. Solar-powered options cost under R300 and require no wiring.
  • Don't advertise on social media that you're away. Post your holiday photos when you get home, not while you're gone.
  • Vary your routine. If you leave and arrive at the same times every day, criminals notice. Small changes in your schedule make you less predictable.
  • Keep valuables out of sight. Don't leave laptops, phones, or bags visible through windows or in parked cars.
  • Know your neighbours. Neighbourhood watch isn't old-fashioned — it's one of the most effective community security tools in South Africa.
  • Get a dog. Any dog that barks at strangers is a deterrent. Size doesn't matter as much as noise.
  • Display security signage. Visible signs and stickers from a security company make criminals think twice — even before you have a full system installed.

The best security is layered — combine these habits with cameras, an alarm system, and armed response for proper protection.

How to Protect Your Retail Store from Shoplifting and Theft

12 May 2025

Retail crime costs South African businesses billions of rands annually. If you run a shop, warehouse, or any business with stock, you need a security plan. Here's what works.

CCTV coverage is non-negotiable

Cameras should cover every aisle, the till area, all entry and exit points, and the storeroom. Visible cameras deter casual shoplifters. Hidden cameras catch organised theft rings and internal shrinkage.

Access control for the storeroom

Most internal theft happens when employees or unauthorised persons access the storeroom without oversight. Electronic access control with audit logs tells you exactly who went in and when.

Alarm system with after-hours monitoring

Break-ins typically happen overnight or over weekends. A monitored alarm system with armed response means criminals have minutes — not hours — before someone arrives on scene.

Staff training

Your staff are your first line of defence. Train them to recognise suspicious behaviour, how to handle a situation safely, and when to call for help rather than confront someone directly.

Security patrol for shopping centres

If your store is in a mall or business park, security patrol adds an additional layer. Regular, visible patrols deter smash-and-grab incidents and keep the area safer for both staff and customers.

Load Shedding

Does Load Shedding Leave Your Security System Vulnerable?

5 May 2025

The honest answer: it can, if your system isn't set up properly. When the power goes off, a basic alarm or CCTV system with no battery backup goes offline — no cameras recording, no alarm sensors active, no connection to the monitoring centre. Criminals know this, and break-in rates spike during scheduled outages.

Battery backup (UPS)

Every alarm panel should have a battery backup that keeps the system running for at least 4 to 8 hours. At EE Solutions, we install backup batteries as standard with every alarm system.

CCTV with battery or solar backup

Cameras connected to a UPS or dedicated battery pack continue recording even when Eskom is off. For properties in areas with frequent outages, solar-powered camera solutions are also available.

Cellular communication

Older alarm systems that rely on Telkom landlines lose their link to the control room during outages if the phone exchange loses power. Modern systems use cellular (SIM card) connectivity, which operates independently of the electricity grid.

Electric fencing

Electric fencing energisers should be connected to a battery backup. Most quality energisers have built-in batteries, but they degrade over time — it's worth checking that yours is still holding charge.

What you should do

  • Ask your security provider to test your backup batteries — they degrade over time and may need replacing.
  • Upgrade to cellular communication if you're still on a landline connection.
  • Consider a dedicated security UPS that covers your alarm panel, router, and at least two cameras.

Do You Really Need Security Patrols?

28 Apr 2025

If you live in a housing estate, manage a business park, or run a commercial property, security patrols are worth considering. Here's why they work.

Visible deterrence

The presence of uniformed security personnel reduces crime. Burglars, vandals, and opportunistic criminals avoid areas where they might be seen and intercepted. A patrol car or officer on foot changes the risk calculation for anyone planning a break-in.

Patrols catch what cameras miss

CCTV is excellent for monitoring and evidence, but cameras have blind spots and can't physically respond. A patrol officer on the ground spots suspicious activity — a car circling the block, someone loitering near a wall, a gate left unlocked — and acts on it immediately.

Response time drops

When patrol officers are already in the area, response to an alarm activation or emergency call is measured in minutes, not in the time it takes to dispatch someone from across town.

When do you need patrols?

  • Residential estates and complexes — regular perimeter and internal patrols keep residents safe and property values stable.
  • Business parks and office buildings — after-hours patrols prevent break-ins and vandalism.
  • Retail centres — visible security reduces shoplifting and makes customers feel safe.
  • Events — temporary event security manages crowds, controls access, and handles incidents.

What good patrol looks like

  • Officers are PSIRA-registered and vetted
  • Patrol routes are varied and not predictable
  • GPS tracking confirms patrols are actually happening
  • Incident reports are provided regularly
